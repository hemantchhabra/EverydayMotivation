# The Crystal Conductor

**Pairing:** Quartz Crystal as Concert Conductor

## The hook
A precisely cut sliver of quartz, when given a tiny electric voltage, bends and rebounds via the piezoelectric effect (discovered by the Curie brothers in 1880) at exactly 32,768 Hz — 2¹⁵ cycles per second — and that single, tuning-fork-shaped crystal is the steady downbeat behind every quartz watch since Seiko's Astron (1969) and most digital clocks on Earth.

## Visual brief

- **Dimensions:** 1080 × 1350 (portrait, social poster)
- **Palette:**
  - `#0B0F1A` — concert-hall ink (background)
  - `#F3F1E8` — quartz facet white (the crystal itself / display type)
  - `#A98BD6` — amethyst violet (secondary facets / accent lines)
  - `#E3B23C` — citrine warm gold (the role tag, baton tip, "32,768 Hz" number)
  - `#7A8190` — cool stage gray (grid, secondary type, scale rule)
- **Mood:** precise, hushed, crystalline
- **Key visual elements:**
  - One focal object — a six-sided quartz prism with a hex pyramid termination — drawn cleanly as a vector with two visible facets shaded in white and violet, positioned as the conductor's baton, oriented along an upward diagonal.
  - Concentric oscilloscope rings emanating from the crystal at its hertz value, each ring labelled with a power-of-two division step (32,768 → 16,384 → … → 1 Hz), making the math of the tick legible.
  - A single oscillating waveform line crossing the lower half — the "tempo" the conductor keeps.
  - Strong type lockup bottom-left: large italic display title, uppercase ROLE tag in citrine, single-line hook in cool white. Aligned to a strict 80-px margin grid; not centered.
- **Motion:** the crystal pulses on a spring easing once per second (the divided-down beat); each pulse fires a single oscilloscope ring outward, and a clean sine wave traces left-to-right under the title. Calm, ~5-second loop.
