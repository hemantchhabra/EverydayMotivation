# The frog that stops its heart for winter

**Pairing:** Wood Frog (*Rana sylvatica*) as Cryogenicist

## The hook
Alaskan wood frogs survive winters as low as −18 °C by allowing roughly 65 % of their body water to freeze into extracellular ice — the liver dumps stored glycogen as glucose (concentrations rising to ~100× baseline) which floods every cell as a natural cryoprotectant, while the heart stops, breathing stops, and neural activity ceases for weeks at a time (Costanzo, do Amaral, Rosendale & Lee, *Journal of Experimental Biology* 216, 2013, 3461–3473). In spring the animal thaws from the inside out, the heart restarts on its own, and the frog hops away.

## Visual brief
- **Dimensions:** 1080 × 1350 (portrait).
- **Palette:**
  - `#0B1721` — deep midnight (background)
  - `#152332` — cold-blue specimen board
  - `#B9DDEB` — frost ice-blue (primary accent, from real hoarfrost)
  - `#E8F4FA` — ice highlight (glow)
  - `#A26A38` — wood-frog dorsal tan
  - `#5B3E22` — wood-frog eye-mask / dark blotches
  - `#F5C264` — glucose amber (cryoprotectant callout)
- **Mood:** hushed, clinical, resolute.
- **Key visual elements:** a top-down "specimen jar" hexagonal chamber holds a single crouched wood frog in warm tan tones — the only warm colour in the piece. Hoarfrost dendrites grow inward from the hexagon walls toward the frog, freezing it in place. Perimeter annotations mark the four vitals that make this survivable: `−6 °C`, `♥ 0 BPM`, `65 % H₂O → ICE`, `GLUCOSE ×100`. Fusing the pairing: the medical/cryo-lab typography and the frost geometry frame the frog exactly the way a cryogenicist frames a specimen — the animal *is* the experiment.
- **Motion:** hoarfrost dendrites nucleate at the hexagon walls and grow inward with a spring-eased dash reveal over ~2.6 s while the temperature counter drops from `0 °C` to `−6 °C` and the ECG line traces one final normal beat, then flatlines.
