# Dung Beetle, Astronomer

## The Scientific Hook

The African dung beetle (*Scarabaeus satyrus*) is the only insect — and one of very few non-human animals — known to navigate by the Milky Way itself. On moonless nights, it uses the polarized band of galactic starlight as a celestial compass to roll its dung ball in a perfectly straight line away from competitors at the pat (Dacke et al., *Current Biology*, 2013). A creature whose entire world is a sphere of dung happens to be a working field astronomer, taking sightings of our home galaxy to do its job.

## Visual Brief

**Dimensions:** 1080 × 1350 px (4:5 portrait, social-first)

**Color palette:**
- Deep galactic indigo `#0B1437`
- Stardust silver `#D8DCE8`
- Warm beetle-carapace black `#141215` with iridescent green-violet shimmer (`#3E7B5C` → `#6E3A8A`)
- Dung-ball umber `#6B4A2B`
- Faint ochre horizon `#8B6F3A`

**Mood:** Quiet, reverent, comedic-sublime. The smallness of the laborer against the enormousness of the sky. Patient, nocturnal, dignified — not a joke, a vocation.

**Key visual elements:**
- A single dung beetle in low three-quarter profile, atop its perfectly spherical ball, antennae raised toward the zenith.
- The Milky Way arcing diagonally across the upper two-thirds of the frame, rendered as a soft polarized band rather than a literal scatter of stars — wide-field, painterly, slightly motion-streaked.
- A faint dotted sight-line from the beetle's eye to the galactic core, the way an astronomer's diagram traces a bearing.
- Below the beetle, a savanna horizon reduced to a single ink-line; the dung ball casts a long, starlit shadow that runs dead-straight across it.
- Optional micro-detail: a brass-toned sextant motif subtly embossed in the dung ball's texture, readable only on close inspection.
