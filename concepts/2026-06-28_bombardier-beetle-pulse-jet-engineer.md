# The Beetle With A Buzz Bomb

**Pairing:** Bombardier Beetle (*Brachinus* spp.) as Pulse-Jet Engineer

## The hook
When startled, a bombardier beetle pumps hydroquinones and hydrogen peroxide from a storage reservoir into a chitin-walled reaction chamber lined with catalases and peroxidases; the exothermic oxidation flashes the mixture to ~100 °C and ejects it as 500–1000 discrete pulses per second — the same firing pattern Dean et al. (*Science* 248, 1990) explicitly identified as a "biological pulse jet."

## Visual brief

- **Dimensions:** 1080 × 1350 (portrait, social poster)
- **Palette** (drawn from *Brachinus crepitans* coloration + reaction-flash imagery):
  - `#0A0B10` — deep carbon (background / negative space)
  - `#E74218` — pronotum / spray flash orange-red
  - `#F6A45A` — heat-glow ember
  - `#1C2638` — elytra metallic blue-black
  - `#A0A6B2` — instrument gray (technical labels, grid)
- **Mood:** clinical, dangerous, precision-engineered
- **Key visual elements:**
  - A side-elevation engineering plate of the beetle, abdomen tilted up, drawn as a cutaway technical schematic. The two glandular chambers — reservoir and reaction chamber — are visible in the cutaway with thin dimension lines pointing to labels.
  - A staccato sequence of pulsed spray jets ejecting from the abdomen tip, each pulse a distinct hot-edged droplet, fading along the trajectory.
  - Asymmetric layout: beetle anchored to the upper-right of the plate, type lockup pinned to the lower-left, leaving negative space for the spray to cross the frame diagonally.
- **Motion:** the spray pulses fire at a slowed-down rhythm (~6 visible pulses per loop), each launched on a spring-easing burst with a brief incandescent flash at the nozzle; trajectory arcs fade by the time they reach the page edge. 5-second loop.
